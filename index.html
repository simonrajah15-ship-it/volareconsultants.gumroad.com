Free Tip for Parents

The single most useful thing you can do this week: ask your child to explain to you what they think AI actually is — in their own words. Don't correct them. Just listen. What they say will tell you exactly where their understanding is strong and where the gaps are. Most children describe AI as "smart" or "like a brain." That's the starting point this programme builds from.

The AI Future Your Child Is Growing Into

Three things most parents don't know yet.

Not to frighten you. Because if nobody tells you now, the world will tell your child later — without preparation.

01 — The Jobs Reality

By the time your 10-year-old enters the workforce, an estimated 40–50% of today's job tasks will be automated by AI. The jobs that survive will not reward those who can do the work — they will reward those who can think about the work, question it, and improve it. Memorisation and routine execution are already losing value. Critical thinking and creativity are not.

02 — The Trust Reality

Children who grow up using AI without understanding it develop a dangerous habit: they trust confident-sounding output without questioning it. AI hallucinates — it states false information with the same tone as true information. A child who cannot spot this is not just academically vulnerable. In a world where AI-generated content is everywhere, the inability to question is a life skill deficit.

03 — The Identity Reality

Gen Alpha is the first generation to grow up with AI as a social presence — not just a tool. AI companions, AI tutors, AI-generated friendships. Children who have not been taught where human connection ends and artificial simulation begins are at real risk of preferring the frictionless comfort of AI over the necessary difficulty of real relationships. This is already happening. It is not a future concern.

This Month's Family Conversation

One question. Tonight. No preparation needed.

Ask this at dinner, in the car, or before bed. Listen more than you speak. The conversation that follows will tell you everything about where your child's AI understanding currently stands.

Ages 8–11

"If AI can answer any question instantly — why do you think we still need to learn things ourselves?"

Don't give them the answer. Sit with the silence. What they say next is their real starting point.

Ages 11–14

"If a company could hire an AI to do your dream job for less money — what would make them hire you instead?"

This question is not hypothetical for this age group. It is their reality in 8–10 years. Hear how they think about it now.

Ages 14–17

"In 10 years, who do you think will control the most powerful AI systems — and does that concern you?"

Teenagers who can think about power, ownership, and consequence are the ones who will shape AI — not just be shaped by it.
